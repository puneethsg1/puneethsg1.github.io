Research Program

Host–Microbe Interactions in Early Life

My research focuses on understanding molecular and immunological mechanisms governing host–microbe interactions during early life.

I study how maternal immunity, microbial genetics, microbiome dynamics, and pediatric nutrition shape immune development and susceptibility to infection in neonates and young infants.

Conceptual Framework

At a Glance

Biological Focus

Host–microbe interactions at mucosal surfaces during early life.

Systems Studied

Maternal immunity, neonatal microbiome, microbial genetics, and nutrition.

Translational Goal

Prevent neonatal infectious disease through mechanism-based interventions.

Research Areas

My work integrates immunology, microbiology, genetics, and nutrition to define mechanisms of colonization, immune development, and infectious disease susceptibility in early life.

Immunity

Maternal Immunity and Neonatal Microbiome

Investigating how maternal antibodies regulate microbial colonization, mucosal immunity, and immune development during early life.

Pathogenesis

Microbial Determinants of Pathogenesis

Identifying microbial genes and pathways that control colonization, virulence, persistence, and immune modulation in the developing host.

Host Response

Microbiome–Immune Interactions

Studying how microbial communities influence immune signaling pathways, barrier function, and host defense mechanisms at mucosal surfaces.

Genomics

Functional Genomics of Microbial Virulence

Using CRISPR-based and genetic approaches to identify bacterial genes involved in colonization, pathogenesis, and host adaptation.

Nutrition

Nutrition, Microbiome, and Early-Life Disease

Examining how pediatric nutrition and diet-associated microbial changes influence immune development, microbial ecology, and susceptibility to neonatal infectious disease.

Translation

Prevention of Neonatal Infection

Integrating immunology, microbiology, genetics, and nutrition to identify preventive strategies that improve outcomes in vulnerable infants.

Research Vision

My long-term goal is to establish an interdisciplinary research program that defines molecular mechanisms governing host–microbe interactions and translates these discoveries into strategies for preventing infectious diseases during early life.

Define Mechanisms

Dissect how maternal immunity, microbial factors, and host signaling pathways shape neonatal susceptibility to infection.

Integrate Nutrition

Determine how nutritional exposures influence the microbiome, immune maturation, and resilience to infectious disease in infancy.

Translate Discovery

Develop mechanistic foundations for vaccines, microbiome-based therapeutics, and preventive strategies for neonatal and pediatric infections.

Future Lab

The Gopalakrishna Laboratory

The Gopalakrishna laboratory will study how microbes interact with the immune system at mucosal surfaces and how these interactions influence susceptibility to infection during early life.

The lab will integrate microbiology, immunology, genetics, nutrition, and translational approaches to define disease mechanisms and identify preventive interventions for neonatal and pediatric infectious diseases.
